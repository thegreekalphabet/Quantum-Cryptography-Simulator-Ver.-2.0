What is Quantum Cryptography?

Quantum cryptography uses the principles of quantum mechanics to provide unbreakable encryption. Unlike classical cryptography, which relies on mathematical complexity, quantum cryptography's security is guaranteed by the laws of physics themselves.

The BB84 Protocol

Our simulator implements the BB84 protocol, the first quantum cryptography protocol, which uses quantum properties of photons to generate a secure encryption key between two parties (traditionally called Alice and Bob).

Quantum Properties

We use two quantum properties: superposition (a particle existing in multiple states simultaneously) and the uncertainty principle (measuring one property affects other properties). These make it impossible for an eavesdropper to intercept the communication without being detected.

How Our Encryption Works

1. Quantum State Preparation

The sender (Alice) prepares qubits in random quantum states using two different bases:

  • Rectilinear Basis (↑↓): Represents 0 and 1
  • Diagonal Basis (↗↙): Represents + and -

2. Quantum Transmission

The receiver (Bob) measures each qubit using randomly chosen bases. Due to quantum mechanics:

  • Correct basis: Measurement gives correct value
  • Wrong basis: Measurement gives random result

3. Key Sifting

Alice and Bob:

  • Compare their chosen bases publicly
  • Keep only the bits where they used the same basis
  • These matching bits become their shared secret key

4. Message Encryption

The final steps:

  • Message is converted to binary
  • Secret key is used to encrypt the binary message
  • Same key is used for decryption at the other end

Security Features

Eavesdropper Detection

Any attempt to intercept the quantum transmission will disturb the quantum states, making the interference detectable.

Perfect Forward Secrecy

Each communication session uses a new random key, ensuring that compromising one message doesn't affect others.

Quantum-Safe

The security relies on fundamental physics, making it immune to advances in computational power, including quantum computers.
